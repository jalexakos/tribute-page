J.R.R. Tolkien

The Hobbit and The Lord of the Rings

Fantasy Writer, Professor (1892-1973)

10 Things You Probably Didn't Know About J.R.R. Tolkien

  • By the age of four, Tolkien could read and write fluently. This was clearly a foreshadowing of his incredible talent for languages, cultures, and mythologies.
  • He was a soldier in the First World War, and even fought in the infamous Battle of the Somme. This experience, which for most soldiers was profoundly troubling, deeply shaped his conscience and overall sense of right and wrong. It also played a role in his distrust of industrialization.
  • His travels greatly influenced his writing. Specifically, his travels in Switzerland, and the profound beauty of the country, played a role in the landscape aesthetics of the elves in his books.
  • His relationship with his wife, Edith, inspired his characters Beren and Lúthien, whose love story played an important role in his legendarium. Beren was a mortal man and Lúthien an immmortal Elf, which some believe represented the disapproval Edith's family felt about their marriage (as Tolkien was Catholic and her family was not).
  • Tolkien learned almost twenty languages in his lifetime, including Medieval Welsh. His passion for languages and cultures is reflected in the care that went into his world-building in his fantasies, as he crafted entire languages (fourteen of them!) and histories for the different races (men, elves, dwarves, and more) in his stories.
  • Tolkien was part of a literary society called "The Inklings". Consisting of himself, fellow fantasy writer and professor C.S. Lewis, poet and novelist Charles Williams, and philosopher and novelist Owen Barfield, this group meet in a pub in Oxford with great frequency. They discussed numerous topics, and read aloud pieces of their work as they were developing them.
  • While in the midst of grading papers one day, the famous first words of The Hobbit came to him. “In a hole in the ground lived a hobbit”.
  • In 1972, Tolkien was given the Commander of the Order of the British Empire for his works in literature by Queen Elizabeth II. The Order "is a British order of chivalry, rewarding contributions to the arts and sciences, work with charitable and welfare organisations, and public service outside the civil service."
  • Many of Tolkien's unpublished works and notes were published posthumously by his son, Christopher. Christopher, a passionate fan and lover of the mythos, carefully edited the works before publishing them.
  • Both The Hobbit and The Lord of the Rings were turned into blockbuster trilogies, directed by Peter Jackson. Both starred Christopher Lee as the powerful wizard Saruman. Lee was not only a great actor, but had actually met Tolkien personally many years prior, and was a huge fan of his work.
